Ships in Chinese seas have vanished from industry tracking systems, adding to the global supply chain's problems. China's growing isolation from the rest of the world, along with a rising aversion to foreign influence, could be to blame.
According to analysts, the drop-off in shipping traffic began at the end of October, as China prepared to pass data privacy legislation.
Because ships are equipped with an Automatic Identification System, or AIS, transceiver, shipping data providers can usually track them all over the world.
This system uses high-frequency radio to let ships to communicate information such as position, speed, course, and name to stations located along coastlines. Information can be exchanged by satellite if a ship is out of range of those stations.
However, this is not the case in China, the world's second-largest economy and a key player in global trade. According to statistics from worldwide shipping data service VesselsValue, the number of vessels sending signals from the country has dropped by about 90% in the last three weeks.
"We're witnessing an industry-wide decline in terrestrial AIS signals in China right now," said Charlotte Cook, VesselsValue's lead trade analyst.
