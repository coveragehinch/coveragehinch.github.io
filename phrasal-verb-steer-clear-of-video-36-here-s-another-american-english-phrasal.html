“steer clear of”

The phrasal verb “steer clear of” is used when we want to stay out of the way of a problem or any sort of negative situation; like maybe a mean, nasty person. We could say “You’d better STEER CLEAR OF anyone in that family.” Rumor has it, that they have a lot of connections with organized crime.

Here are a few examples of how to use “STEER CLEAR OF” in sentences:

If you have any sense at all, “you’ll STEER CLEAR OF my neighbor” when he’s on one of his drinking binges.

“You should STEER CLEAR OF that airline company.” The only reason their tickets are so cheap is because they have the worst safety record.

You’re lucky to be alive! “I told you to STEER CLEAR OF that part of town after dark.” I really don’t know what’s going on in your head sometimes.

If you don’t want to lose all your money, “you’d better STEER CLEAR OF these Internet get-rich-quick promises.” What sounds like a great way for you to get rich, is really just a scam for someone else to get rich by taking your money. Don’t be a dummy.

“I’m going to STEER CLEAR OF those mountains during the winter.” I’ve heard too many stories about people who went in them and never came out.

“I suggest you STEER CLEAR OF that used car salesman.” He’s cheated more people out of their money than you can imagine.

“Son, you’d better STEER CLEAR OF that woman.” She’s nothing but trouble. You could fill a baseball stadium with all her broken-hearted ex-boyfriends.
